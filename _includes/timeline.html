The Journey: How I Taught Myself Design

Travel. Read. Research.

After leaving my previous company, I spent a few months traveling and volunteering. While taking the time to cross off things on my bucket list, I started by doing readings and researches on UX and UI design.

Make S.M.A.R.T Goals. Execute My Study Plan.

With advice from Karen, who taught herself design in 6 months and landed a job at a startup, I started to execute my own study plan.

This post by a product designer at Pinterest helped me fine tune my study on UX/UI.

Start With The Foundation: Graphic Design

  • Drew 1 hour everyday to practice my sketching.
  • Took the course on Coursera to build a foundation on color, typography and designing by grid.
  • Read smashing magazine to keep up with the latest trend and techniques.

Learn By Practice: UX/UI and Interaction Design

  • Started with Don't Make Me Think! to get the right mindset in good user experience practices.
  • Took the course on Coursera to learn methodology on user experience research and prototyping.
  • Learnt the basic and advanced techniques in Interaction Design on Lynda.com to make effective, engaging and interactive design.
  • Re-designed existing websites and mobile apps. Practice is the best way to learn design.

Learn From The Professionals

  • Went to Dribbble for inspiration on some of the best designers.
  • Read through iOS Human Interface Guidelines and Google Material Design for mobile design practice.
  • Met up with designers from UXSG and learn from the fellow professionals.

Design With Codes: HTML, CSS and JavaScript

  • Learnt front-end website design on Coursera, and it was amazing to see the colorful codes being converted into responsive website.
  • Built a portfolio website on Jekyll, and shared the template and source codes on GitHub.

Keep Learning

Apart from drawing and designing, I spend 1 to 3 hours daily on reading to keep my skills sharp.

  • Follow design blogs and articles on Medium, Google Design, Think with Google and smashing magazine and many more.
  • Get design inspiration on Dribble, Behance and Little Big Details.
  • Read books on design. Although this reading list was made 7 years ago, it is still quite relevant today.
  • Keep writing weekly.
